Understanding Knee Osteoarthritis

A comprehensive look at causes, non-surgical treatments, and preventative measures.

What Exactly is Knee Osteoarthritis?

Osteoarthritis (OA) is a degenerative joint disease. Over time, the protective cartilage that cushions the ends of your bones wears down. In the knee, this means the thigh bone (femur) and shin bone (tibia) may begin rubbing directly against each other. This friction causes significant pain, swelling, and a severe loss of joint mobility.

While often associated with age, OA can affect adults at any stage of life, particularly following severe joint injuries or due to genetic predispositions.

Notable Symptoms to Watch For

  • Pain: Aching that worsens after activity or at the end of the day.
  • Stiffness: Particularly noticeable in the morning or after resting for a long period.
  • Crepitus: You might feel a grating sensation or hear popping/crackling when using the knee.
  • Bone Spurs: Hard lumps forming around the joint.
  • Reduced Flexibility: Inability to move the knee through its full range of motion.

Effective Prevention & Non-Surgical Methods to Solve

While osteoarthritis cannot be "cured" once the cartilage is lost, there are several highly effective methods to halt its progression, manage the pain, and maintain mobility.

Weight Management

Every pound of body weight places three to six pounds of pressure on the knees. Losing even 10 pounds can significantly relieve joint stress and slow degradation.

Physical Therapy

Strengthening the muscles around the knee (the quadriceps and hamstrings) makes the joint more stable and decreases the direct impact on the bone.

Low-Impact Exercise

Avoid running or high-impact jumping. Instead, focus on swimming, cycling, or elliptical training which builds cardiovascular health without damaging cartilage.

Medication & Injections

NSAIDs (like Ibuprofen) can reduce inflammation. For severe pain, doctors may recommend localized Corticosteroid or Hyaluronic acid injections to temporarily lubricate the joint.

When to Seek Surgical Options

If non-surgical treatments fail and pain severely restricts your daily activities, it's time to consult an orthopedic surgeon. Knee replacement (arthroplasty) is a highly successful procedure where damaged bone and cartilage are replaced with metal and plastic parts.
